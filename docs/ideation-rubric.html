Proposal scoring rubric

Author

Jason Wallin, OEDA-GIS Manager

Background

Proposals for OEDA projects will be written by the OEDA economist and researchers, generally in collaboration with researchers, data experts, or subject matter experts from partner agencies.

These proposals will be scored using a rubric. Ideally, this rubric reflects the value that OEDA can bring to state government and the values that OEDA brings to our work.

Some of the rubric items will likely be scored by OEDA researchers and presented to the Advisory Council. For others, the Advisory Council will decide how to score them during a meeting.

The rubric will be used to make go/no-go decisions about projects and help set priorities for the OEDA portfolio.

The rubric items appear as the numbered items below.

Process value: Ethical

  1. One of the following must be true:
    1. The proposal provides reasoning that the project does not meet the federal definition of research, so IRB review is not required
    2. The proposal provides reasoning that the project is research, but does not include human subjects, so IRB review is not required
    3. The proposal is human subjects research, and meets one or more of the requirements for review by the Public Health Institutional Review Board and has received approval from that body
    4. The project is human subjects research, does not meet any of the requirements of (c), above, and provides a checklist or attestation from someone other than the investigators that the research is exempt from IRB review
    5. The project provides documentation of approval from an IRB

See details, below

Details

Ethical

The proposal provides reasoning that the project does not meet the federal definition of research, so IRB review is not required

Research means a systematic investigation, including research development, testing, and evaluation, designed to develop or contribute to generalizable knowledge [45 CFR 46.102(l)]

Many common state agency analytics projects involve development and evaluation, but are nonetheless not research. This is generally because they are not designed to develop or contribute to generalizable knowledge. That is, they are not seeking findings that can be applied to populations or situations beyond those studied.

Typical systematic uses of data that are not research include:

  • Internal program evaluation and quality improvement efforts
  • Operational activities
  • Informational interviews

The proposal provides reasoning that the project is research, but does not include human subjects, so IRB review is not required

Human subject means a living individual about whom an investigator (whether professional or student) conducting research: (i) Obtains information or biospecimens through intervention or interaction with the individual, and uses, studies, or analyzes the information or biospecimens (ii) Obtains, uses, studies, analyzes, or generates identifiable private information on identifiable biospecimens 45 CFR 46.102(e)

Often, work with administrative data is neither the result of intervention nor interaction.

Intervention includes both physical procedures by which information or biospecimens are gathered (e.g., venipuncture) and manipulations of the subject or the subject’s environment that are performed for research purposes. 45 CFR 46.102(e)(2)

Generally, researchers for state agencies are not able to create randomized control trials where Oregonians’ experiences with their government are manipulated to develop strong internal validity for generalizable claims.

Interaction includes communication or interpersonal contact between investigator and subject 45 CFR 46.102(e)(3)

Researchers for state agencies are often working with private information.

Private information includes information about behavior that occurs in a context in which an individual can reasonably expect that no observation or recording is taking place, and information that has been provided for specific purposes by an individual and that the individual can reasonably expect will not be made public (e.g., a medical record).

But, researchers can often answer meaningful agency questions without using identifiable private inforamtion.

Identifiable private information is private information for which the identity of the subject is or may readily be ascertained by the investigator or associated with the information.

Analyses using deidentified data, which cannot be reasonably reidentified by the investigator, are generally not considered human subjects, as individual humans can no long be connected to the attributes being studied.

Creating analytical datasets which follow Safe Harbor Regulations is one way to ensure that the analysis does not involve human subjects.

The proposal meets one or more of the requirements for review by the and has received approval from that body

Any study that meets any of the criteria below must seek review by the Public Health Institutional Review Board.

• The research is sponsored by the PHD (Oregon Public Health Division) or MCHD (Multnomah County Health Department);
• The research is conducted by or under the direction of an employee or agent of PHD or MCHD in connection with his/her agency responsibilities, or using any property or facility of PHD or MCHD;
• The research involves the use of PHD or MCHD’s data;
• The research involves the use of PHD or MCHD’s non-public information to identify or contact human research subjects or prospective subjects;
• Funding for the research will be handled through PHD or MCHD, but the research will be done at another location
Public Health Institutional Review Board (p. 3)

The project is human subjects research, but provides a checklist or attestation from someone other than the investigators that the research is exempt from IRB review

While the Federal HHS does not require it, they strong recommend that determinations of exempt status be conducted by individuals other than the investigators.

For context, the PH and MCHD Institutional Review Board requires expedited review by the Board to determine whether a protocol is exempt.

Exempt research includes (among other exemptions):

  • Research in educational settings involving normal educational practices 45 CFR 46.104(d)(1)
  • Research whose interactions are only educational tests or observation of public behavior 45 CFR 46.104(d)(2)
  • Research whose interventions are benign behavioral interventions 45 CFR 46.104(d)(3)
  • Secondary research for which consent is not required 45 CFR 46.104(d)(4), including specifically the following

The research involves only information collection and analysis involving the investigator’s use of identifiable health information when that use is regulated under 45 CFR parts 160 and 164, subparts A and E, for the purposes of “health care operations” or “research” as those terms are defined at 45 CFR 164.501 or for “public health activities and purposes” as described under 45 CFR 164.512(b);
